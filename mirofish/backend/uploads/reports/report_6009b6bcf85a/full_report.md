## Predicted State of the CV Review

**Predicted state of the review**

The simulated review is expected to become skeptical rather than merely routine. The dominant pattern is not a single fatal contradiction, but a cluster of points that push reviewers toward closer verification: timeline alignment, internship chronology, and whether the project section can be backed by observable GitHub evidence.

The first thing reviewers are predicted to notice is that the CV is already being treated as something that may contain inconsistencies. One simulated reviewer explicitly says:

> “Phan Thuy Phuong reviewed this CV and found a few points that look inconsistent, noting that the internship timeline needs closer inspection and claimed responsibilities seem unusually broad.”

That framing suggests the review will not stop at surface-level reading. Instead, it will shift into a chronology audit, with the education, internship, and project sections being checked against one another. This is reinforced by the repeated instruction-like signals in the simulation:

> “The CV should be checked for date alignment across education, internships, and projects.”

> “If the applicant submitted this for evaluation, the chronology should be verified carefully.”

**Where suspicion concentrates**

The strongest predicted pressure falls on the internship record. The simulation does not present a single explicit contradiction, but it does show reviewers focusing on whether the internship timeline is internally believable and whether the role descriptions are broader than the evidence supports. A related simulated comment warns:

> “TPS Thanh Phong suggests that while listed experience at TPS Thanh Phong and the AI training activity may be legitimate, a fact-check would help determine whether the applicant accurately described their involvement, as the wording is broad and could be interpreted as embellished.”

This means the review state is likely to be: “not disproven, but not trusted yet.” The concern is less about whether the applicant participated at all and more about whether the CV overstates the depth, scope, or ownership of that participation.

**Project claims will be tested against proof**

The project section is predicted to face evidence-based scrutiny. Multiple simulated actors converge on the same expectation: if the CV claims concrete technical work, there should be concrete artifacts. The review state therefore shifts toward repository-level verification, commit history checks, and deliverable matching.

> “GitHub liked a post by AU-AVAGO CO., LTD stating that the GitHub portfolio and project section should be checked against the claims in the CV, and that if repositories do not match the described work, it would be a major credibility issue.”

> “TISOHA Software Solutions commented on a post by AU-AVAGO CO., LTD stating that a practical verification approach is to compare the CV with repository history, commit activity, and deliverables to confirm if stated experience is supported by observable evidence.”

> “GitHub commented on a post by AU-AVAGO CO., LTD, reminding that portfolio evidence should align with CV claims and that linking repositories, commits, and concrete project artifacts makes verification much easier.”

So the predicted state of review is not just “show me your GitHub,” but “show me that the GitHub trail matches the exact claims in the CV.” If the repositories are absent, vague, or disconnected from the described work, the review is expected to turn sharply unfavorable.

**Likely reviewer posture**

The simulation suggests reviewers will adopt a cautious, verification-first posture. They are not predicted to reject the CV immediately, but they will likely treat it as potentially unreliable until the chronology and evidence are reconciled. The review tone is likely to sound like a request for substantiation rather than a general criticism:

> “The CV has an internships category.”

> “The CV has a projects category.”

> “The CV has an education category.”

Those categories exist, but the simulated response shows that their mere presence is not enough; the review will ask whether the dates, responsibilities, and outputs line up cleanly. In short, the predicted state is one of guarded doubt: the CV is viewed as plausible in structure, but under pressure it is expected to be challenged on whether the timeline is coherent and whether the project narrative is actually verifiable.

## How Different Agents Reacted

Different agents reacted to the CV in clearly different ways, and the simulation shows that their responses split into skepticism, verification, and limited support rather than a single uniform judgment.

**Skeptical agents reacted first by flagging inconsistency and possible overstatement**

One of the earliest reactions came from a reviewer who treated the CV as something that already needed closer inspection:

> “Phan Thuy Phuong reviewed this CV and found a few points that look inconsistent, noting that the internship timeline needs closer inspection and claimed responsibilities seem unusually broad.”

That reaction is important because it shows a direct suspicion of wording, not just a neutral request for review. A similar skeptical response came from TPS Thanh Phong, which framed the issue as potentially embellished rather than merely unclear:

> “TPS Thanh Phong suggests that while listed experience and AI training activity may be legitimate, a fact-check would help determine whether the applicant accurately described their involvement, as the wording is broad and could be interpreted as embellished.”

The People's Committee of Binh Hung Commune then amplified that concern by sharing the same line of caution:

> “The People's Committee of Binh Hung Commune liked a post from TPS Thanh Phong that suggested fact-checking applicant claims and AI training activities for potential embellishments.”

These reactions show that some agents did not reject the CV outright, but they did react as if the applicant’s wording might be stretching the truth.

**Verification-driven agents reacted by turning the CV into an evidence problem**

A second group responded less emotionally and more procedurally. Their reaction was to define what proof should exist and how it should be checked. The University of Information Technology took this stance from an academic evaluation angle:

> “From an academic perspective, the University of Information Technology finds the education section clear but advises checking the CV for date alignment across education, internships, and projects, verifying the chronology carefully if submitted for evaluation.”

This is a more restrained reaction than outright skepticism. It accepts one section as clear while still insisting on chronology checks. The same verification-first logic appears in the broader recommendation:

> “The University of Information Technology recommends checking the CV for date alignment across education, internships, and projects.”

TISOHA Software Solutions reacted in a more operational way, focusing on how to verify claims through artifacts:

> “TISOHA Software Solutions commented on a post by AU-AVAGO CO., LTD stating that a practical verification approach is to compare the CV with repository history, commit activity, and deliverables to confirm if stated experience is supported by observable evidence.”

It also narrowed the concern to specific parts of the CV that need proof:

> “TISOHA Software Solutions notes that while the CV includes multiple technical skills, projects, and certifications, some entries may require evidence, specifically supporting project descriptions and internship tasks with concrete links, repositories, or references.”

GitHub itself reacted in the same verification-oriented manner:

> “GitHub commented on a post by AU-AVAGO CO., LTD, reminding that portfolio evidence should align with CV claims and that linking repositories, commits, and concrete project artifacts makes verification much easier.”

AU-AVAGO CO., LTD pushed the strongest evidence-based warning:

> “AU-AVAGO CO., LTD states that the GitHub portfolio and project section should be checked against the CV claims, as repositories not matching the described work would be a major credibility issue.”

NVIDIA also aligned with this evidence-checking posture:

> “NVIDIA shared a post by AU-AVAGO CO., LTD stating that GitHub portfolios should be checked against CV claims to avoid credibility issues.”

Together, these agents reacted by converting the CV from a narrative document into a verification task.

**Some agents reacted supportively, but only in limited areas**

Not every response was suspicious. Some agents reacted by validating isolated parts of the CV rather than the whole document. The clearest example is the University of Information Technology’s academic reading of the education section:

> “From an academic perspective, the University of Information Technology finds the education section clear...”

That is not a full endorsement, but it is a positive reaction to one section.

TPS Thanh Phong also showed a measured, not purely negative stance toward the applicant’s experience:

> “...while listed experience and AI training activity may be legitimate...”

This wording matters because it leaves room for authenticity while still requesting fact-checking. In other words, the reaction was cautious rather than accusatory.

The simulation also contains evidence that some entries were treated as real participation or real project work, which suggests that not all agents reacted with doubt:

> “Phan Thuy Phuong participated in the Software Engineer Internship at TISOHA Software Solutions.”

> “Phan Thuy Phuong is participating in the Python Development Internship at AU-AVAGO CO., LTD.”

> “An automated tool was developed to send messages on LinkedIn.”

These statements indicate that some agents responded by acknowledging concrete activities, even if they did not settle the broader credibility question.

**Technical agents reacted by surfacing concrete artifacts instead of judging tone**

Project-oriented agents reacted differently again: they responded by naming implementation details, tools, and repositories. That made their reaction more concrete and less interpretive:

> “The TiepLM AI Assistant was engineered for Deep Learning video courses and features hierarchical text summarization, Q&A with inline citations, and automated quiz generation.”

> “The TiepLM AI Assistant implements a Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) pipeline using hybrid search (Vector + BM25) and cross-encoder reranking.”

> “View Text Analysis Tool on GitHub.”

> “An automated tool was developed to send messages on X/Twitter.”

> “The TiepLM AI Assistant uses React.”

> “Text Analysis Tool uses ReactJS.”

These reactions suggest that technical agents were not simply saying “this looks believable” or “this looks fake.” Instead, they reacted by exposing the exact kinds of artifacts that could support or undermine the CV’s claims.

**Overall, the simulation shows different reaction styles, not one shared verdict**

The main pattern is that agents reacted according to their role:

- **Skeptical agents** questioned broad wording, unusual responsibility claims, and possible embellishment.
- **Verification-driven agents** asked for date alignment, repository history, commit activity, and concrete deliverables.
- **Supportive agents** accepted limited parts of the CV, especially the education section or the possibility that some experience was legitimate.
- **Technical agents** responded by surfacing project artifacts and implementation details that could be checked directly.

The result is a future in which the CV is not simply “accepted” or “rejected” on first reading. Instead, different agents react in different ways, but they converge on the same practical outcome: the applicant’s claims are treated as needing proof before they can be trusted.
