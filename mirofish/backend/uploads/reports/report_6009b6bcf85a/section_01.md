## Predicted State of the CV Review

**Predicted state of the review**

The simulated review is expected to become skeptical rather than merely routine. The dominant pattern is not a single fatal contradiction, but a cluster of points that push reviewers toward closer verification: timeline alignment, internship chronology, and whether the project section can be backed by observable GitHub evidence.

The first thing reviewers are predicted to notice is that the CV is already being treated as something that may contain inconsistencies. One simulated reviewer explicitly says:

> “Phan Thuy Phuong reviewed this CV and found a few points that look inconsistent, noting that the internship timeline needs closer inspection and claimed responsibilities seem unusually broad.”

That framing suggests the review will not stop at surface-level reading. Instead, it will shift into a chronology audit, with the education, internship, and project sections being checked against one another. This is reinforced by the repeated instruction-like signals in the simulation:

> “The CV should be checked for date alignment across education, internships, and projects.”

> “If the applicant submitted this for evaluation, the chronology should be verified carefully.”

**Where suspicion concentrates**

The strongest predicted pressure falls on the internship record. The simulation does not present a single explicit contradiction, but it does show reviewers focusing on whether the internship timeline is internally believable and whether the role descriptions are broader than the evidence supports. A related simulated comment warns:

> “TPS Thanh Phong suggests that while listed experience at TPS Thanh Phong and the AI training activity may be legitimate, a fact-check would help determine whether the applicant accurately described their involvement, as the wording is broad and could be interpreted as embellished.”

This means the review state is likely to be: “not disproven, but not trusted yet.” The concern is less about whether the applicant participated at all and more about whether the CV overstates the depth, scope, or ownership of that participation.

**Project claims will be tested against proof**

The project section is predicted to face evidence-based scrutiny. Multiple simulated actors converge on the same expectation: if the CV claims concrete technical work, there should be concrete artifacts. The review state therefore shifts toward repository-level verification, commit history checks, and deliverable matching.

> “GitHub liked a post by AU-AVAGO CO., LTD stating that the GitHub portfolio and project section should be checked against the claims in the CV, and that if repositories do not match the described work, it would be a major credibility issue.”

> “TISOHA Software Solutions commented on a post by AU-AVAGO CO., LTD stating that a practical verification approach is to compare the CV with repository history, commit activity, and deliverables to confirm if stated experience is supported by observable evidence.”

> “GitHub commented on a post by AU-AVAGO CO., LTD, reminding that portfolio evidence should align with CV claims and that linking repositories, commits, and concrete project artifacts makes verification much easier.”

So the predicted state of review is not just “show me your GitHub,” but “show me that the GitHub trail matches the exact claims in the CV.” If the repositories are absent, vague, or disconnected from the described work, the review is expected to turn sharply unfavorable.

**Likely reviewer posture**

The simulation suggests reviewers will adopt a cautious, verification-first posture. They are not predicted to reject the CV immediately, but they will likely treat it as potentially unreliable until the chronology and evidence are reconciled. The review tone is likely to sound like a request for substantiation rather than a general criticism:

> “The CV has an internships category.”

> “The CV has a projects category.”

> “The CV has an education category.”

Those categories exist, but the simulated response shows that their mere presence is not enough; the review will ask whether the dates, responsibilities, and outputs line up cleanly. In short, the predicted state is one of guarded doubt: the CV is viewed as plausible in structure, but under pressure it is expected to be challenged on whether the timeline is coherent and whether the project narrative is actually verifiable.
